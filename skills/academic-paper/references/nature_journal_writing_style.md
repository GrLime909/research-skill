# Journal-Style Writing Notes

> **Purpose**: Writing-level adjustments for specific journal families, adapted from nature-writing. The existing `journal_submission_guide.md` covers **submission logistics** (where to submit, cover letters, required sections). This file covers **how to write** differently for different target audiences. Apply after the draft is complete — re-tune the opening, not the argument.

## Nature (and Nature subjournals)

### Audience
Broad, multi-disciplinary. A reader outside the immediate subfield must grasp the claim, the gap, and the significance from the abstract and the first paragraph of the introduction alone.

### Drafting Priorities
- **Abstract opening**: must signal significance for a non-specialist without overclaiming
- **Summary paragraph**: use a staged funnel — `broad field -> sharper background -> exact problem -> here we show -> what the result changes -> broader context / outlook`
- **Jargon**: avoid any term that would not appear in a typical Nature News piece. Define or replace
- **Word count**: unforgiving. Prefer cuts to compressions when a section runs long
- **Em dashes**: none in body prose
- **Hedging**: avoid both overclaim (`proves`, `definitive`) and timidity (`might possibly suggest`)
- **Significance framing**: if the material does not naturally support broad significance, surface this as a structural issue rather than drafting around it

### Section Format Notes
- Abstract is usually **unstructured prose** (no headed subsections) for many Nature titles
- The gap sentence and the `Here we show` sentence should be explicit and separated in function
- Methods often goes to the end; the main text is significance-driven
- References typically capped (~50 for Articles). Plan citation budget early

### Things to Flag Rather Than Silently Fix
- If the manuscript leans on a specialist mechanism the broad audience cannot follow, flag it
- If significance for a non-specialist is genuinely thin, surface it under `Assumptions or missing inputs`

## Nature Communications

### Audience
Open-access, broader than a subfield journal, more specialist-tolerant than Nature. The reader is an active researcher in an adjacent area.

### Drafting Priorities
- Significance framing matters, but a less aggressive opening than Nature is acceptable
- Tight prose still preferred even though limits are looser
- Methods can be more detailed in-line; reproducibility expectations are explicit
- Same em-dash and hedging defaults as Nature

### Pre-Drafting Word Budget (Articles)

| Section | Suggested budget |
|---------|-----------------|
| Introduction | ~700 |
| Results | ~2,000 |
| Discussion | ~800 |
| Methods | ~1,500 |
| **Total** | **~5,000** (includes Methods) |

For methods-heavy papers, shift toward `~1,200 / 1,800 / 700 / 1,800`. For bio/clinical papers with rich Results, shift toward `~600 / 2,400 / 800 / 1,200`.

### Mandatory Companion Artifacts (plan early)
- **Data Availability statement** with valid, public accession numbers
- **Code Availability statement** for any custom code (Zenodo DOI or equivalent)
- **Reporting Summary** — same rigor as the manuscript
- **Cover letter** (separate upload) — three sentences: what / what's new / why cross-disciplinary

### Display Items
- 10-item cap (figures + tables combined)
- No Extended Data tier — anything beyond 10 goes to Supplementary Information
- Decide upfront which results justify a main figure vs. Supplementary

### Abstract
- 150 words, unstructured single paragraph, no citations
- Spell out abbreviations at first use
- Lead with the finding, not the background — editors triage on the abstract
- Include quantitative results where possible

### Transfer from Nature Draft → Nature Communications
Common rework: (1) reabsorb separate Methods into main text budget, (2) redistribute figures beyond 6 (Nature limit) to 10 or Supplementary, (3) re-tune opening for slightly less aggressive significance framing.

## Generic / Undefined Journal

Apply Nature-leaning structure defaults (argument chain, section jobs) without enforcing strictest length or significance-framing demands. Ask the user before final draft:
- Target journal and section format (structured vs unstructured abstract; word limits)
- Audience breadth (subfield vs broad readership)
- Whether a "Significance" paragraph or "Author summary" is required
- Required submission elements (graphical abstract, highlights, key points)
